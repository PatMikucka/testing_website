Why I Started Making Vegan Candles
(And Why It Changed Everything)

By Lila · March 10, 2026

Five years ago I was standing in the candle aisle of a department store, turning over a jar that cost £40, trying to make sense of the ingredient list. The only thing listed was "fragrance." Just that — one word, covering a cocktail of potentially hundreds of undisclosed chemicals.

I remember feeling strangely annoyed. Not just confused — annoyed. Because I'd been burning candles every evening for years without thinking about what I was actually doing to the air in my home. I'd been feeding my lungs synthetic fragrance and paraffin soot, thinking I was creating a cosy, relaxing atmosphere.

"I wanted to understand what I was breathing. That curiosity changed everything."

The Paraffin Problem

My first rabbit hole was paraffin wax. Paraffin is what most commercial candles are made from — it's cheap, available in huge quantities, and takes fragrance well. It's also a direct by-product of petroleum refining. When it burns, it releases soot and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) including toluene and benzene.

Now, to be fair: a well-made paraffin candle burned in a ventilated room probably isn't going to cause you serious harm. But it's not exactly the "relaxing self-care ritual" it's marketed as, either. I wanted something cleaner.

Discovering Natural Waxes

Soy wax was my first love. It's made from hydrogenated soybean oil, burns much cooler and cleaner than paraffin, and is biodegradable. It also holds scent beautifully and has a gorgeous ivory-white finish when it cools.

Coconut wax came next. It's more expensive, but the burn is exceptional — incredibly slow, creamy, and consistent. For my larger jar candles, it's become the gold standard.

I still use soy for my 6oz jars. The scent throw is incredible and it's the most sustainable option I've found at that price point for customers.

The Fragrance Oil Discovery

Here's something that shocked me when I started: most "natural" candle brands still use synthetic fragrance oils. They'll say things like "inspired by the forest" or "botanical blend" on the label, while the actual scent is entirely synthetic. It's not illegal — it's just misleading.

Essential oils are more expensive, harder to work with, and burn differently to fragrance oils. But they're what nature actually made, and when you smell the real thing against a synthetic version, there's no competition. The real thing has depth, complexity, and warmth that synthetics just can't replicate.

"The first time I poured a lavender candle with real essential oil and lit it in my bedroom, I sat on the floor and just breathed for ten minutes."

Making the First Batch

My first proper batch was a disaster. I got the temperature wrong, the wax cratered as it cooled, the wick was too small and the flame drowned, and the scent throw was basically non-existent. I poured it down the drain and started again.

The second batch was better. The third was good. By batch twenty, I was starting to understand what I was doing.

I gave candles to everyone I knew that Christmas. My friends were kind enough to tell me honestly which ones they loved and which ones they'd used once. That feedback shaped everything that followed.

Why Vegan, Specifically?

Beeswax is often marketed as the premium natural option, and it does burn beautifully. But I'm vegan, and I believe that if I can make something equally good — or better — without involving animals, I should. My soy and coconut wax candles match or exceed beeswax on every metric I care about: burn time, scent throw, cleanliness, and finish.

There's also the scale argument. The candle industry uses enormous quantities of beeswax globally. Soy and coconut are simply more scalable, more consistent, and — with the right sourcing — more sustainable.

Five Years Later

I'm still making candles in small batches. I still pour every single one myself, still label them by hand, still add a handwritten note to every order. As the business has grown, I've been careful not to let it outgrow the thing that makes it worthwhile — the slow, careful, personal approach to making something really good.

If you've ever burned a Lila's candle, thank you. If you haven't yet — I hope you find the one that becomes yours.

— Lila x
